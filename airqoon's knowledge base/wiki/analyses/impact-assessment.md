# Airqoon Impact Assessment

This document synthesizes the overarching impacts of Airqoon's air quality monitoring solutions and associated regulatory practices (like Clean Air Zones) across three core dimensions: Social, Health, and Environmental. It draws upon foundational knowledge from the Airqoon knowledge base, including standards (EPA, DSÖ, EN 17660), public policy (CAZ), and sustainability principles.

---

## 1. Social Impacts

Air quality monitoring serves as a catalyst for societal change, empowering communities and reshaping urban behavior.

- **Public Awareness & Transparency:** The open sharing of the Air Quality Index (AQI/HKİ) categorizes pollution into easy-to-understand color codes (e.g., Green for Good, Red for Unhealthy). This transparency allows citizens, especially sensitive groups, to make informed daily decisions.
- **Behavioral Shifts via Policy (e.g., Clean Air Zones):** Implementations like Clean Air Zones (CAZ) directly influence societal habits. By restricting or charging highly polluting vehicles, communities and businesses are financially and socially incentivized to adopt greener transportation methods (e.g., EVs, public transit, walking). The revenue generated is often reinvested into zero-emission public transport.
- **Citizen Engagement & Feedback:** Tools like the [[wiki/entities/airqoon-map|Airqoon Public Map]] feature citizen reporting mechanisms, transforming the public from passive victims of pollution into active stakeholders in urban environmental management.
- **Educational Environments:** Monitoring in schools (via indoor sensors like [[wiki/entities/unit-m|Unit M]]) addresses the social equity of protecting vulnerable demographics (children). Ensuring healthy indoor air quality (IAQ) directly impacts cognitive development, attendance, and long-term societal well-being.

## 2. Health Impacts

The primary objective of air quality standards (such as the EPA's Primary Standards) is the protection of public health.

- **Acute and Chronic Respiratory Issues:**
  - **Particulate Matter (PM2.5, PM10):** Fine particles bypass the body's natural defenses, penetrating deep into the lungs and entering the bloodstream, causing cardiovascular and respiratory diseases.
  - **Ozone (O3):** Short-term exposure causes throat irritation, coughing, and airway inflammation.
  - **Sulfur Dioxide (SO2):** Can cause wheezing and chest tightness within just 5 minutes of exposure.
- **Protection of Vulnerable Groups:** Strict threshold values exist to protect children, the elderly, and asthmatics. Real-time alerts via systems like [[wiki/entities/airqoon-lens|Airqoon Lens]] enable these groups to avoid exposure during severe pollution events.
- **Indoor Air Quality (IAQ) Health:** Since people spend up to 90% of their time indoors, pollutants like VOCs, Formaldehyde, CO2, and biological agents (bacteria, fungi, viruses) pose significant health risks, ranging from "Sick Building Syndrome" to severe allergic reactions.

## 3. Environmental Impacts

Beyond human health, monitoring mitigates broader ecological degradation and drives corporate sustainability.

- **Ecosystem and Asset Protection:** Secondary air quality standards protect public welfare, preventing damage to crops, vegetation, and building infrastructure (e.g., acid rain caused by SO2 and NO2).
- **Climate Change Mitigation:** Tracking greenhouse gases (like CO2) and understanding the corporate carbon footprint is central to Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) reporting. Accurate emissions data empowers industries to reduce their ecological footprint.
- **Spatial Distribution of Pollution:** Utilizing dense networks and advanced IDW (Inverse Distance Weighting) heat maps allows for the pinpointing of industrial hotspots, fugitive emissions, and early-warning fire detection. This localized data is crucial for preventing widespread environmental disasters.
- **Driving Sustainable Development:** Monitoring forces the adoption of clean energy, sustainable agriculture, and green urban planning, aligning with global frameworks like the European Green Deal and the UN's Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs).
