# Spatiotemporal Assessment of Particulate Matter in an Organized Industrial Zone

**Subtitle**: Using Low-Cost Sensor Networks: A Case Study from İnegöl, Türkiye
**Authors**: Barış Can Üstündağ (Airqoon), Coşkun Ayvaz, Ülkü Alver Şahin (Istanbul University-Cerrahpaşa)
**Event**: ASIC2026

## Overview
This presentation details a comprehensive 2021-2022 study in İnegöl, Türkiye, utilizing a network of 10 Airqoon IoT-enabled solar-powered sensors. The study aimed to quantify PM10 and PM2.5 levels, evaluate the influence of Organized Industrial Zones (OIZs), and analyze wind and morphology effects on pollutant dispersion.

## Key Findings & Context
- **İnegöl Context**: A rapidly growing industrial hub with over 4,000 furniture enterprises (48% of exports). The residential areas sit directly adjacent to industrial zones.
- **Sensor Performance**: Field calibration against BAM regulatory monitors for PM10 showed high reliability: Hourly $R^2 = 0.84$, Daily $R^2 = 0.96$, Daily MAE: $4.5 \mu g/m^3$.
- **High Pollution Levels**: 
  - 100% of sites exceeded the national HKDYY PM10 limit ($40 \mu g/m^3$).
  - The WHO PM2.5 guideline ($5 \mu g/m^3$) was exceeded by 5-11x across ALL sites.
- **Source Attributions (PM Increments above urban background)**:
  - **Residential Heating (HUZ site)**: +111% PM10 | +106% PM2.5 (Driven by solid fuel use in low-income areas).
  - **Industrial OIZ (SIN1 site)**: +65% PM10 | +65% PM2.5 (Metal, textile, and furniture sector emissions).
  - **Traffic (OSM site)**: +43% PM10 | +43% PM2.5 (Exceeds traffic increments seen in Istanbul, Berlin, and London).
- **Seasonal & Diurnal Trends**:
  - Winter PM levels are 2-3x higher than summer levels due to residential heating and secondary aerosol formation.
  - Midday peaks from secondary aerosols, and nighttime peaks from solid fuel combustion.
- **Spatial Heterogeneity**:
  - A weaker inter-site correlation ($R = 0.41$) compared to Istanbul ($R = 0.72$) indicates highly complex local PM variability.
  - Urban morphology and building layouts create stagnation zones causing up to 2x elevated concentrations, proving that distance between sites is not the sole predictor of PM variability.

## Policy Implications
1. **Industrial OIZs**: Represent dominant pollution drivers requiring urgent review of siting and emission controls.
2. **Solid Fuel Heating**: Transitioning low-income areas to clean energy is a critical public health priority.
3. **Traffic Management**: Emissions exceed international benchmarks.
4. **Sensor Networks**: Dense low-cost air quality sensor (LCAQS) networks effectively fill monitoring gaps left by sparse official networks.

## Future Research Directions
- Multi-height sensor deployments in street canyons.
- Real-time meteorological coupling and deposition modeling.
- Indoor-outdoor PM infiltration monitoring for exposure assessment.
- Comparative analysis across other Turkish industrial mid-sized cities.
