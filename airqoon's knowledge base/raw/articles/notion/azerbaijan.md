Air quality in Azerbaijan is deteriorating due to factors such as traffic, oil refinery industry, waste burning, and field clearing. The capital, Baku, situated on the Caspian Sea coast and the Absheron Peninsula, is known for its windy climate, which positively impacts air quality by helping disperse pollution quickly. However, strong winds can also stir up particles from roads and dry areas around the city. Most of the pollution thresholds set by the EU are exceeded in Baku.

## Monitoring System and Infrastructure:

- Since 2021, six automatic online stations have been in use.
- Three automatic stations are installed in Baku, one each in Sumgayit and Ganja. Additionally, there is a mobile laboratory.
- The stations are equipped with devices from the US-based "Thermo" company.
- **By 2025, it is planned to install 25 automatic stations nationwide.** (On December 18, 2020, the Head of the National Hydrometeorology Service, Umayra Taghiyeva, announced this on the [Azerbaijan Republic Ministry of Ecology and Natural Resources website](https://eco.gov.az/az/nazirlik/xeber?newsID=11751)).
- The Khizi fund station in Altiaghaj National Park conducts long-range air pollution monitoring.
- Pollutants measured include PM2.5, PM10, nitrogen dioxide (NO2), nitrogen oxides (NOx), sulfur dioxide (SO2), carbon monoxide (CO), hydrogen sulfide (H2S), ozone, and BTEX gases.

## Pollution Sources and Trends:

- In the 1970s and 80s, industrial facilities were the main polluters (70-80%). Today, the transportation sector is predominant (80%).
- Industrial pollution has decreased due to technological advancements and relocation of industries outside the city.
- Cross-border pollution, especially dust transport from Turkmenistan and the Arabian Peninsula, is a significant factor.
- The number of vehicles in Baku increased from 100,000 in 2000 to 1 million in 2017.
## General Situation and Assessments:

- According to Matanat Avazova, Deputy Head of the Department of National Monitoring on the Environment at the Ministry of Ecology and Natural Resources, the overall atmospheric air condition in the country is described as "satisfactory."
- Major cities like Baku, Sumgayit, and Ganja have higher pollution levels.
- Baku ranked 57th out of 117 countries in the IQAir rankings.
- Extreme pollution events are rare and are usually due to cross-border dust transport.
- The Absheron Peninsula is more affected compared to other regions due to its high population density.
## Issues and Solution Efforts:

- The use of old, high-emission vehicles is a significant issue.
- Efforts are being made to reduce traffic congestion.
- New industrial facilities are opting for low-emission technologies.
- There are plans to make air quality data publicly available.
- Work is ongoing to modernize monitoring systems and data management.

### The project carried out with the Finnish Meteorological Institute (FMI) laid the foundation for today's air monitoring network.

([https://www.eeas.europa.eu/delegations/azerbaijan/air-quality-project-azerbaijan-concluded-successfully_en?s=217](https://www.eeas.europa.eu/delegations/azerbaijan/air-quality-project-azerbaijan-concluded-successfully_en?s=217))

- The Finnish Meteorological Institute (FMI) conducted an air quality monitoring project in Azerbaijan with EU funding.
- The project took place between 2016-2019 with a budget of 1.4 million Euros.
- Project Goal: To modernize the Soviet-era air quality monitoring network and provide reliable data to support decision-making processes.
- Between 2019-2020, five new air quality stations were planned to be established. A total of 25 new stations are planned for the future.
- Over 200 ministry employees received training.
- A **Vaisala air quality sensor** was installed in Azerbaijan by the Finnish company.

## Project Summary

Air quality is a serious problem in Azerbaijan, especially in the capital city of Baku. Daily traffic density in some streets of Baku reaches millions of vehicle passages, significantly impacting air quality. This heavy traffic negatively affects air quality by increasing the emission of particulate matter and harmful gases. Population growth around metro areas, intensive construction, and the decrease in green spaces exacerbate the problem. The dusty structure of the Absheron Peninsula, nearby quarries, and industrial centers like Sumgayit also contribute to air pollution.

The current air quality monitoring network is insufficient. Measurements are only taken at five locations: three in Baku and one each in Sumgayit and Ganja. Important cities like Shirvan and Lankaran lack monitoring networks. This situation does not fully reflect the country's air quality status and makes it difficult to develop effective policies.

We are launching a comprehensive project to address these issues. To protect children's health and raise awareness among future generations, we plan to prioritize placing new air sensors near schools. A total of 20 new air sensors will be installed: 12 in Baku, 4 in Sumgayit, 2 in Ganja, and 2 each in Shirvan and Lankaran.

This approach will allow us to continuously monitor air quality around schools and better understand the pollution levels children are exposed to. It will also provide students and parents with practical learning experiences about environmental awareness. Additionally, it will help us detect air quality differences across various parts of cities, particularly the variations between areas of high traffic density, industrial zones, and residential areas.

## Data Generation Plan

As part of our data generation plan, we will establish a comprehensive air quality monitoring network in various cities of Azerbaijan over a 12-month period. In addition to the existing locations in Baku, we will create new measurement points in Neftçilər, Koroğlu, Sahil, 20 Yanvar, Memar Əcəmi, Gənclik, Hazi Aslanov, Bakikhanov, Hatai, Garaçukur, Hırdalan, and Masazır, areas affected by traffic and various environmental sources. This will provide a detailed reflection of the air quality situation in different parts of the capital. We will also establish measurement points in other important cities across the country: 4 in Sumgayit, 2 in Ganja, and 2 each in Shirvan and Lankaran. A total of 20 new air sensors will be installed, measuring important pollutant levels such as PM2.5, PM10, NO2, SO2, and CO.
The use of low-cost sensors in this project is a strategic choice. This selection allows us to take measurements at more points within budget constraints, enabling us to better understand air quality differences across various parts of cities. Despite their low cost, these sensors have high sensitivity and accuracy, ensuring reliable data collection. The chosen sensors can operate with solar panels and batteries. This feature will allow us to collect uninterrupted data even in areas with limited electrical infrastructure, thus adapting to the country's diverse geographical and infrastructural conditions.
Furthermore, these sensors offer 4G and NB-IoT connectivity options. This technological infrastructure guarantees real-time data transmission and provides remote monitoring and maintenance capabilities. This feature is crucial for ensuring continuous data flow and quickly resolving potential issues. Thus, we will be able to provide a continuous and reliable data stream with minimal human intervention.

## Data-Sharing Plan:

Regarding data sharing, a comprehensive plan will be implemented to increase the project's transparency and encourage widespread use of the data. Data from the sensors will be transmitted to the central server at 5-minute intervals and processed immediately. The data will be presented as structured CSV files compatible with the OpenAQ Platform data format. An API will be provided for developers and researchers, and data will also be visualized through interactive maps and graphs on a user-friendly web portal. All data will be automatically integrated into the OpenAQ platform, and detailed metadata information will be provided for each sensor.

## Positive National-level Impact Plan:

The systemic impact the project aims to achieve at the national level is to create concrete and applicable changes in air quality management in Azerbaijan. In this context, we first aim to establish the country's first comprehensive National Air Quality Monitoring Network by collaborating with the Ministry of Ecology and Natural Resources of the Republic of Azerbaijan. This system will enable real-time monitoring of air quality across the country by integrating data produced by our project and information from existing monitoring stations. In line with the data obtained and international best practices, a comprehensive National Air Quality Action Plan will be developed to improve air quality nationwide. This plan will include short, medium, and long-term goals and will be regularly updated.
In cooperation with local governments, we aim to initiate a "Low Emission Zones" pilot application, especially in Baku where traffic is heavy, based on the data obtained from the project. This application will tighten emission control in high-traffic areas, encourage the use of public transportation, and create pedestrian and bicycle-friendly areas. If successful, this application can be expanded to other major cities.
An important feature of our project is the positioning of measurement points around schools. This strategic choice will contribute to raising awareness about air quality among both children and parents.
