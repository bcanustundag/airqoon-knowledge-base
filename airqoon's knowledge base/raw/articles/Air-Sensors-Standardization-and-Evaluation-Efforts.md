# Air Sensors Standardization and Evaluation Efforts

Air pollution is one of the most crucial environmental issues we face today. This problem has a broad impact, triggering climate change, facilitating the spread of diseases, adversely affecting human health, reducing average life expectancy, and causing negative effects on plant cover and ecosystems. Monitoring air quality enables the understanding of this issue, evaluating its impacts, and developing effective solutions. However, it is paradoxical that countries with the highest levels of air pollution often lack adequate air quality monitoring infrastructure.

Without fundamental data on air quality, conducting further research on the effects of air pollution, encouraging citizens to advocate for clean air actions, and formulating policies to address the most significant environmental threat to human health becomes challenging. Globally, reference measurement stations used in air monitoring are systems with installation and operation costs that are quite high. These stations face a series of challenges, including difficulties in capturing real-time data, experiencing high data loss, and being impractical to set up everywhere due to their size and cost. In African, Asian, and Latin American countries, the number of these measurement systems is almost negligible, resulting in significant gaps in air quality data. Insufficient funding for air pollution exacerbates the limitations in effective interventions for air quality management, increasing the need for new measurement systems.

In the past decade, rapidly advancing air sensors have played a crucial role in filling gaps in air quality data. These sensors stand out for being more accessible and cost-effective compared to traditional reference measurement stations. Through these sensors, the opportunity for air quality monitoring has expanded in broader geographic areas worldwide, and particularly in the United States and Europe, significant reductions in data gaps have been achieved. However, the increased demand and usage, coupled with many companies entering the production of air sensors, have raised concerns about the accuracy of the data generated by these sensors. In this context, various guides and reports have emerged to provide guidance on the suitability and reliability of sensors.

### **Air Sensors in the USA**

In the United States, civil society organizations play an active role in sensor usage. Measurement of air pollution is often driven by the initiatives of local communities and individuals, with an increasing demand dynamic moving from the public towards governing layers during this process (Yatkın, 2023).

The U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) does not consider sensor data as official data but finds these data beneficial for non-regulatory, supportive, and informational monitoring (NSIM) applications. The Advanced Air Sensor Guide published by the EPA in 2022 states that sensors can be used as part of emergency interventions, such as measuring increases in air pollution during wildfire events, to help officials and residents understand exposure and take appropriate actions, including monitoring near suspected sources. Another significant application is the deployment of sensors to locations missed by reference-class stations to support increasing the number of data points that can be collected (EPA, 2022).

In February 2021, the EPA released two air sensor performance target reports, one for O3 sensors and the other for PM2.5 sensors. The purpose of these reports is to provide a consistent set of test protocols, metrics, and target values for evaluating the performance of air sensors, particularly for non-regulatory supplementary and informational monitoring (NSIM) applications, used outdoors and in fixed locations. The reports include protocols for two testing scenarios (EPA 2021):

- Basic testing, including field assessment
- Enhanced testing, including laboratory assessment

### **Air Sensors in Europe**

In the realm of air pollution monitoring in Europe, both reference-class stations and numerous municipal projects have integrated sensors into existing systems. This integration allows for obtaining a broader dataset and more comprehensive monitoring of air pollution levels. In contrast to the widespread use of personal exposure sensor systems in the United States, there is a significant demand in Europe from local governments to measure urban air quality (Yatkın, 2023). Particularly, the London municipality has embraced sensor technologies rather than creating a new reference network. In this context, the Breathe London project has emerged as a prominent example, highlighting efforts to monitor and improve air quality in the city.

Gas sensors are defined as measurement devices for "indicator measurements" in the European Air Quality Directive. According to the Technical Report on the Review of Sensors for Air Quality Monitoring published by the European Union in 2019, a sensor system can be considered "equivalent" when it meets the defined Data Quality Objectives (DQOs). For sensor system measurements to be included in the legal framework set by the European Air Quality Directive, these sensors must meet one of the DQOs. The DQOs, defined as the allowed maximum relative uncertainty, are specified for reference and indicative measurements or objective estimates (European Commision, 2019).

The sensor classes specified in the Performance Assessment of Air Quality Sensor Systems (CEN/TS 17660-1:2021) published by the Council of Europe in 2021 are:

- Class 1 and Class 2 sensor systems for regulatory or other purposes that fulfil the DQO for respectively indicative measurements and objective estimation as defined in Directive 2008/50/EC;
- Class 3 sensor systems for non-regulatory purposes that have relaxed target measurement uncertainty requirements, e.g. for specific research topics, educational purposes and citizen science.

The fundamental lack of data related to air quality poses a significant obstacle to understanding and addressing the global air pollution problem. One factor that further complicates this issue is inadequate funding. Efforts to combat air pollution strive to attract only 1% of international development funds. Within charitable funds, this percentage drops to a mere 0.1% (Clear Air Fund, 2023).

According to a report released by EPIC in 2023, a metric called "Opportunity Score" was developed to identify where gaps in PM2.5 data infrastructure exist and to pinpoint the most valuable locations for filling these gaps (Hasenkopf, Sharma, Kazi, Mukerjee, & Greenstone, 2023). The key factors in this metric include population, annual average PM2.5 concentration, and the total number of monitoring stations. Larger countries such as India or China have lower opportunity scores because the likelihood of another monitoring study being effective at the national level in these countries is low. As a result of this scoring, 46 countries were identified as "High Opportunity" countries. Finland, already one of the countries meeting WHO guidelines, has more monitors per capita for its population, which is 1/152 of the 46 countries
