# Introduction

Air pollution is one of the most significant environmental health risks today. Health effects vary according to the concentration of the pollutants in the air and their duration. It is necessary to determine the pollution level so that pollutants do not adversely affect the environment and human health. At present, low-cost air sensors are gaining more and more attention than traditional air pollution monitoring methods, and their development continues to expand and evolve rapidly.


The EPA refers to the term 'air sensor' as a class of non-regulatory technology that is low-cost, portable, capable of measuring several pollutants simultaneously, and often easier to operate than regulatory stations. For example, monitoring air pollution with reference measurement methods (regulatory stations) requires skilled operators to maintain and calibrate measuring instruments. On the other hand, air sensors describe the hardware and software set that can be operated without human intervention and enable unskilled users to monitor air pollution without additional technical knowledge.



Quality assurance (QA) and quality control (QC) are the most critical components of an air pollution monitoring campaign that will make sure that useful and meaningful data are collected. QA consist of planning steps to manage the campaign and collect, evaluate and review the sensor data. QC procedures are activities that include colocation, data correction (air sensor system calibration), maintenance, data validation and review.

1. Sensor Unit Preparation: Sensor units are prepared according to the needs of the measurement campaign.
1. Zero-Pollutant Test: Units are tested in zero-air conditions before starting a co-location experiment.
1. Co-location: Units are deployed on a reference station for a month.
1. Calibration & Validation: Both measurements are aggregated in the cloud to build and validate the calibration model. For every pollutant and sensor unit, a unique calibration model is trained and used to asses the best-in-class accuracy performance.
1. OTA Parameter Set: Calibration parameters are set over-the-air for every individual sensor unit.
1. Field Deployment: Calibrated units are deployed according to the [getting started guide](/59955bbd4e334a669f9b6fbbe238eb6b).
