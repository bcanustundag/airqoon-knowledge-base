What This T ells Us
Weaker inter-site correlation (R = 0.41) than Istanbul (R = 0.72) confirms 
more complex local PM variability in İnegöl.
Spatial heterogeneity is driven by local emission sources — industrial 
zones, traffic, and solid fuel heating — not regional background transport.
Unlike São Paulo, Beijing, or Augsburg, inter-site distance is NOT a 
predictor of PM variability in İnegöl — local morphology dominates.

Key T akeaways & Policy Implications
Industrial OIZs are dominant pollution drivers
Sinanbey (SIN1) recorded 65% above background PM. 
Industrial siting and emission controls need urgent review.
Solid fuel heating creates extreme winter peaks
HUZ concentrations reach 3× summer levels. Transitioning 
low-income areas to clean energy is a public health priority.
Traffic emissions exceed international benchmarks
PM₂.₅ increment at OSM (10 µg/m³) surpasses Istanbul, 
Berlin, London. Urban traffic management is critical.
Dense LCAQS networks fill monitoring gaps
Low-cost sensors effectively resolve fine-scale pollution 
gradients that sparse official networks miss.
Urban morphology governs exposure
Building layout and wind corridors create stagnation zones 
with up to 2× elevated concentrations.

Conclusions & Future Directions
Key Conclusions
▸ All sites exceed national (HKDYY) and international (WHO) PM 
limits
▸ Industrial OIZs, solid fuel heating & traffic are the three 
dominant source classes
▸ Winter-to-summer ratio of 2–3× confirms seasonal 
combustion as a major driver
▸ Spatial heterogeneity is locally controlled — not distance-
dependent
▸ Dense LCAQS networks are a scalable tool for industrial zone 
monitoring
Future Research
▸ Multi-height sensor deployments in street canyons & 
courtyards
▸ Real-time meteorological coupling & wet/dry deposition 
modeling
▸ Indoor–outdoor PM infiltration monitoring for exposure 
assessment
▸ Comparative analysis across Turkish industrial mid-sized cities
▸ Linking PM data to health outcomes for evidence-based policy
